The Gap Year: Learning to Synthesize

Published: June 2026 · 5 min read · By Hafiz Muhammad Saeed

In the middle of 2023, I made a choice that felt counter-intuitive to almost everyone around me. Fresh off completing my FSc Pre-Medical studies—a route that was supposed to lead straight into medical school admission cycles—I stepped away. I chose to take a gap year. It was not a year of leisure, nor was it a structured pre-university program. It was a blank canvas of twelve months, and I decided to fill it with craft.

I spent my days doing things that, on the surface, had absolutely nothing to do with each other. I bought a set of wood carving chisels and spent hours chipping away at small blocks of pine, learning how the blade must negotiate the direction of the grain. I bought yarn and taught myself crochet, figuring out the geometry of loops and rows. I worked with modeling clay, folded complex origami structures, and tried my hand at learning new languages.

"In a world that demands specialization from day one, taking a step back to explore unrelated crafts can feel like a distraction. In reality, it is where synthesis begins."

The Anatomy of Tactile Craft

When you sit down with a raw block of wood, you cannot force your will upon it. Pine has a structure. It has soft rings and hard rings, knots, and cracks. If you carve against the grain with too much pressure, the wood splits, ruining hours of work. You have to develop a dialogue with the material.

Crochet taught me a different lesson—one of absolute precision and logic. Every stitch is a mathematical unit. If you miscount a single loop in row three, the error will lay hidden until row ten, where the fabric suddenly begins to warp and warp. To fix it, you have to rip out hours of progress back to the source of the mistake.

These tactile endeavors were not just hobbies; they were mental training. They demanded deep focus, respect for constraints, and the patience to refactor your own mistakes.

From Wood to Code: Learning Python

In the final months of my gap year, I decided to teach myself programming. I picked up Python, writing my first basic scripts in a text editor.

As I learned about functions, loops, and conditional data flows, I felt an immediate familiarity. Writing code did not feel like a foreign language; it felt exactly like wood carving and crochet.

  • Constraints: A compiler enforces syntax rules just as wood grain enforces carving angles.
  • Modularity: Creating reusable functions is like designing a standard crochet stitch pattern to build a larger blanket.
  • Refactoring: Re-writing bad code is the digital equivalent of undoing warped clay or picking apart rows of yarn to fix a structure.

I realized that software is simply another medium of craft. Instead of timber or yarn, your raw material is logic. And like any material, it requires patience, alignment, and respect for its inherent structure.

The Power of Synthesis

Taking a gap year to be a generalist gave me a perspective that I carry into my Computer Science studies at UET Taxila. I do not look at programming as just a set of toolsets or frameworks. I see it as architectural construction.

When I design backend MVC database models or outline fleet structures for a C++ system, I am drawing from the same structural discipline I developed while folding origami or carving wood. Synthesis—the ability to connect the logic of code with the tactile patience of physical craft—is my primary builder tool. It teaches me to appreciate the foundation, to write code from scratch, and to value clean, quiet architecture.
